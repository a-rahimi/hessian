# Newton on CIFAR-10: summary so far

## The fitting problem

We are training a sequential dense network on CIFAR-10 to test whether the Hessian-inverse-vector trick (linear-time second-order optimizer) actually accelerates learning compared to plain SGD. The architecture is `SequenceOfDenseBlocks` with `--num-layers 8 --hidden-dim 24 --activation relu`, applied to CIFAR-10 images resized to `--image-size 16` and flattened. The training loss is cross-entropy over 10 classes; the per-step training batch is 64 samples by default. We track two metrics: per-step batch loss (averaged over a 10-step trailing window as `train_loss_avg10`) for optimization progress, and a fixed 256-sample held-out probe batch for `probe_loss` and `probe_accuracy` (generalization). This anchor model was selected in [Phase 4](results.md) as the smallest ReLU configuration where SGD reaches meaningfully better than chance — narrower configurations (hidden_dim=8) saturated dead ReLUs and never trained.

## SGD performance

SGD is the bar. At the anchor model with `--lr 0.1 --num-steps 1000` on full CIFAR-10, SGD reaches `probe_loss=1.9712` and `probe_accuracy=0.281` — well below the chance plateau of `log(10) ≈ 2.30`. SGD on a single 64-sample batch held fixed for 1000 steps depends sharply on lr: at lr=0.1 it can only reach min loss 1.64 (essentially the chance plateau on that one batch) because the step overshoots, but at lr=0.01 it drives the same fixed-batch loss to **0.19** with batch accuracy 0.95 ([exp-048b](runs/exp-048b-sgd-reuse-batch-lr0.01/)) — the model can fully memorize 64 samples when the step size is right. At lr=0.001 SGD stalls at 2.15 because the step is too small to make meaningful progress within 1000 steps. So the optimizer-side floor on this problem is at most 0.19 on a single batch and 1.97 on full CIFAR-10; anything above is the optimizer's failure, not the problem's.

## Newton performance and everything we tried to close the gap

Newton, in every configuration we have tested, fails to reach SGD's loss. On full CIFAR-10 trailing-avg training loss plateaus in the 2.22–2.30 range; on a single fixed batch the best Newton has reached is min loss 1.16 ([exp-053](runs/exp-053-newton-memorize-lr0.5-lm/), `ε=0.5 lr=0.5` with LM-adaptive ε) versus SGD's 0.19. We swept the static knobs broadly: ε across {0.01, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0, 5.0} and lr across {0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.3, 0.5, 1.0}; the best static recipe was `ε=0.5 lr=0.1` ([exp-028](runs/exp-028-newton-15-frozen-low-eps/)), but it still plateaus around 2.22–2.27 at any num-steps tried (15, 60). We tested every adaptation strategy we could enumerate: LM ε adaptation alone (exp-049: avg10=2.30), LM ε plus plateau-triggered lr halving on a trailing-loss tolerance (exp-050: avg10=2.29), per-step coupling of both knobs to the LM accept/reject signal (exp-051: avg10=2.29) — none changed the plateau by more than mini-batch noise. We switched the LM check from "same-batch" to "fresh-batch" (exp-042) which cleaned up the per-batch overfitting bounces seen in exp-039 but did not lower the plateau. We tried `--max-step-norm 1.0` to clip large excursions (exp-041); it eliminated the bounces but also eliminated the descent. We tested doubling batch size to 128 (exp-032, exp-047) hoping to reduce per-batch Hessian-estimator noise — modest improvement at most. We ran the fixed-batch diagnostic (exp-046, then SGD comparison exp-048/048b) which conclusively showed the wall is Newton-specific, not intrinsic to the problem. Most recently we are sweeping toward larger Newton step magnitudes on the fixed batch: exp-052 at lr=0.01 stalled at 2.02, exp-053 at lr=0.5 reached 1.16, exp-054 at lr=1.0 is in flight. The cumulative picture is that adjusting ε and lr within the standard Levenberg-Marquardt-style recipe cannot make Newton match SGD on this problem; the remaining candidate explanations are structural — indefinite Hessian eigenvalues that ε damping does not properly handle, or non-quadratic local geometry that Newton's trust-region approximation poorly fits — and would require deeper algorithm changes (saddle-free Newton, Gauss-Newton, or curvature-aware trust region) rather than further hyperparameter tuning.
